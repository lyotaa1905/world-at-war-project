Realistic Weapon Representation

Call of Duty: World at War includes many historically accurate weapons such as the M1 Garand, MP40, and Mosin-Nagant rifle. These weapons are modeled visually and functionally to resemble their real-world counterparts. The inclusion of these firearms reflects an effort by the developers to create an authentic World War II experience. This level of detail helps players understand the types of technology used during the war.

Simplification of Combat

While the weapons themselves are relatively accurate, the way they are used in the game is simplified. Real-world combat is far more chaotic and unpredictable than what is portrayed. In the game, players can quickly recover from injuries, carry multiple weapons, and engage in continuous combat without long-term consequences. This creates a more accessible and enjoyable experience but reduces realism.

Exaggeration of Player Ability

The game places the player in the role of a highly capable soldier who can survive intense combat situations. In reality, survival rates in such scenarios were much lower, and soldiers often relied heavily on teamwork and strategy. The game exaggerates individual ability, which shifts the focus from collective warfare to personal action.

Limited Representation of War's Consequences

One of the biggest inaccuracies in World at War is its limited portrayal of the long-term consequences of war. The game focuses on action and immediate outcomes but does not fully explore the lasting effects of warfare, such as trauma, loss, and destruction. This omission simplifies the reality of war and presents a more controlled and contained version of events.

Overall Evaluation

Overall, World at War provides a partially accurate representation of World War II technology. While the weapons and environments are designed to reflect historical reality, the gameplay simplifies and exaggerates many aspects of combat. This balance between realism and entertainment allows the game to be engaging while still loosely grounded in historical technology.
